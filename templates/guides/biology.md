# Biology flashcard writing guide

This guide supplements `CARD_STANDARD.md` and `AUTHORING_PLAYBOOK.md`. It
targets an introductory, college-level path that develops
biological literacy and prepares the learner for deeper molecular, organismal,
and ecological study.

## Organizing principles

Every deck should repeatedly connect content to five crosscutting ideas:

1. evolution;
2. structure and function;
3. information flow, exchange, and storage;
4. pathways and transformations of energy and matter;
5. systems and interactions across scales.

Cards should also practice scientific competencies: forming testable claims,
interpreting data, quantitative reasoning, modeling, experimental design,
evaluating evidence, and explaining how observations distinguish competing
hypotheses. These priorities follow the NSF/AAAS *Vision and Change* framework
and its BioCore/BioSkills elaborations.

## Biology-specific card portfolio

### Mechanism cards

Prefer causal chains over isolated vocabulary.

```markdown
Q: Why does increasing membrane cholesterol buffer fluidity across temperatures?
A: Cholesterol restrains phospholipid movement at high temperature but prevents tight packing at low temperature, reducing both extremes of fluidity.
```

Split a long pathway into cards for the input/output, location, regulatory step,
and causal purpose. Do not ask for an entire pathway as one unordered list.

### Structure–function translation

Use figures to ask the learner to identify how a visible structural feature
supports a function. Useful targets include membranes, organelles, proteins,
nucleic acids, tissues, phylogenies, food webs, and feedback loops.

The front should not contain a fully labeled diagram if labeling is the target.
Use consistent orientation and scale bars where scale matters. Never rely on
color alone to distinguish structures.

### Experimental reasoning

Biology cards should train controls and inference, not merely name methods.

```markdown
Q: Cells receive a drug dissolved in DMSO. What control isolates the drug's effect?
A: Treat otherwise identical cells with the same DMSO concentration but no drug.
```

Ask what result supports a hypothesis, which variable is manipulated, which
control is missing, and what conclusion the data do *not* justify. Distinguish
correlation from causation and direct evidence from inference.

### Quantitative and data cards

Include graph and table interpretation, probability in inheritance, exponential
growth, diffusion scaling, enzyme kinetics, population change, and uncertainty.
Ask for a prediction before calculation. Define axes, units, samples, and error
bars. Do not turn every data card into arithmetic; model selection and trend
interpretation are often the real targets.

### Comparison and misconception cards

Use contrasts that cause genuine interference: prokaryote/eukaryote,
mitosis/meiosis, transcription/translation, genotype/phenotype,
homology/analogy, respiration/breathing, energy/matter, and adaptation/acclimation.
Ask for the decisive distinction in a context rather than memorizing two long
definitions independently.

## Accuracy rules

- State the level of biological organization and organism when generalizations
  have exceptions.
- Distinguish a simplified introductory model from universal truth.
- Avoid teleology: traits do not arise because organisms need them.
- Evolution acts through population-level changes across generations, not an
  individual's effort or intention.
- Energy flows and changes form; matter cycles. Organisms do not create energy.
- A gene does not map one-to-one to a trait in general. Include regulation,
  environment, interactions, and probability where they matter.
- Diagrams of cells, molecules, and anatomy must not imply false relative scale.
- Use current nomenclature and verify medical or health claims against an
  authoritative source. Do not turn the deck into individualized medical advice.

## Prerequisite policy

Teach chemical bonds, water, pH, concentration, macromolecules, free energy,
and probability before depending on them. Introduce an organelle or molecular
actor before asking the learner to predict its perturbation. A failed
application card should reveal a reasoning gap, not an omitted definition.

## Figure priorities

High-value original visuals include:

- molecule-to-cell scale comparisons;
- membrane transport and concentration gradients;
- structure–function comparisons;
- pathway inputs, outputs, compartments, and regulation;
- chromosome behavior across mitosis and meiosis;
- gene-expression and inheritance models;
- experimental setups with controls;
- phylogenetic trees;
- feedback loops, food webs, and matter cycles;
- authentic graphs with uncertainty.

Photographs are useful when morphology, tissue organization, organismal
variation, or field identification is the target. Use openly licensed images
with attribution; do not use decorative stock imagery.

Make a visual-opportunity inventory for every chapter. Treat scale, molecular
or anatomical structure, causal pathways, compartment boundaries, chromosome
states, experimental setups, authentic data, phylogenies, and ecological
networks as separate candidate retrieval roles rather than collapsing them into
one representative figure.

Use clozes only for compact, uniquely cued terminology or relationships after
the underlying mechanism has been introduced. Do not use clozes to memorize an
unexplained vocabulary list, a whole pathway, or a sequence whose causal order
should instead be reconstructed.
