# Mathematics flashcard writing guide

This guide supplements the universal card standard and authoring playbook. It
contains reusable mathematical judgment, not a fixed curriculum from arithmetic
through graduate study. The subject roadmap chooses the learner's actual path.

## What competence looks like

A mathematics deck may need distinct retrieval targets for:

- precise definitions, including examples and non-examples;
- theorem hypotheses, conclusions, and boundary cases;
- choosing a method from structural cues;
- carrying out short, meaningful steps fluently;
- explaining why a step or theorem applies;
- reconstructing a proof's key idea;
- translating among symbolic, numerical, graphical, geometric, and verbal
  representations;
- estimating, checking, and finding counterexamples.

SRS maintains these components. It does not replace sustained proof writing,
multi-step problem sets, derivations, computation, or mathematical exploration.

## Prerequisite and notation policy

State the expected mathematical maturity and allowed tools. Establish notation,
definitions, and simple instances before using them inside a theorem or
application. Do not invoke calculus, linear algebra, probability, or proof
techniques merely because an expert finds them natural.

Every formula card defines its symbols and domain. Include hypotheses that
change truth or method: continuity, nonzero denominators, branch choices,
orientation, independence, convergence, finiteness, and similar restrictions.

## Numerical, symbolic, and conceptual practice

Use numbers when arithmetic or procedural fluency is the target. Use variables
when algebraic structure, generality, or method transfer is the target. Often a
sequence works best:

1. concrete example to expose the pattern;
2. symbolic relation with conditions;
3. method-selection prompt;
4. varied application;
5. contrast with a nearby but inapplicable method.

Do not make symbolic form a universal virtue. A learner who recognizes a
formula but cannot compute a representative case lacks fluency; a learner who
can repeat arithmetic but cannot identify the structure lacks transfer.

## Theorems and proofs

Do not memorize a textbook proof line by line. Separate:

- what the theorem says and why each hypothesis matters;
- the proof strategy or decisive construction;
- local lemmas or transitions worth retrieving independently;
- a reconstruction prompt for the argument's spine;
- boundary tests, counterexamples, and alternative conditions.

For a novice, show an analyzed proof before asking for reconstruction. Then fade
support. Keep full proof writing outside SRS when a card would fragment the
reasoning into meaningless line completions.

## Problem cards

Use IPEE when classification and checking are part of the skill:

- **IDENTIFY** the mathematical object or problem class;
- **PLAN** the theorem, representation, construction, or algorithm;
- **EXECUTE** only the steps needed for this target;
- **EVALUATE** by substitution, inverse operation, dimensions when applicable,
  limiting behavior, a bound, derivative, special case, or counterexample.

Fade the scaffold after the method is established. A problem whose solution is
too long for quick review belongs in external practice; cards can maintain its
method triggers and fragile subskills.

## Interleaving and discrimination

After separately introducing neighboring methods, mix them so the learner must
choose rather than repeat the last procedure. High-value contrasts include:

- necessary versus sufficient;
- pointwise versus uniform;
- independent versus mutually exclusive;
- element versus subset;
- equality versus equivalence or isomorphism;
- local versus global conclusions;
- a theorem and a tempting look-alike whose hypotheses fail.

Ask for the decisive cue or a counterexample. Do not interleave before the
learner has a minimally stable model of each method.

## Figures and representations

Use original diagrams and graphs when visual structure is part of the target:

- geometric constructions and invariants;
- function shape, transformations, slopes, areas, and asymptotes;
- vector, matrix, transformation, and subspace relationships;
- combinatorial objects and graph structure;
- probability distributions and sampling behavior;
- topology, partitions, and set relationships.

Label axes and scale honestly. Do not let a sketch imply a proof, a special case
imply a theorem, or a rendered graph hide domain restrictions. Front figures
must not display the requested label or construction.

## Accuracy and audit checks

- Test claims with examples and adversarial boundary cases.
- Verify algebra and arithmetic independently of prose.
- Distinguish definition, theorem, convention, heuristic, and intuition.
- Preserve quantifier order and logical direction.
- Check whether multiple conventions exist and state the one in use.
- Flag diagrams that suggest false scale, continuity, orientation, or
  dimensionality.
- Treat a plausible but unproved generalization as incorrect, not pedagogical
  shorthand.
