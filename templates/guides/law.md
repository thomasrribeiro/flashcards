# Law flashcard writing guide

This guide supplements the universal card standard and authoring playbook. It
applies to legal education, legal reasoning, and admissions-test preparation.
The deck contract must say which of those it serves; they are not interchangeable.

## Jurisdiction, authority, and time

Every substantive-law deck states its jurisdiction, governing date, intended
learner, and whether it teaches majority rules, a particular code, or a specific
course convention. A rule without jurisdiction and date may be actively
misleading.

Distinguish:

- constitution, statute, regulation, case law, rule, and secondary authority;
- binding from persuasive authority;
- holding from dicta;
- rule from policy rationale;
- current law from historical doctrine;
- black-letter simplification from a contested or fact-sensitive standard.

Verify unstable law against official current sources during every substantive
audit. Cards are educational material, not individualized legal advice.

## What competence looks like

Useful legal retrieval includes:

- identify the issue raised by legally significant facts;
- state a rule with elements, exceptions, and burden where relevant;
- map facts to each disputed element;
- generate the strongest argument and counterargument;
- distinguish a neighboring doctrine or procedural route;
- identify authority, procedural posture, remedy, and standard of review;
- predict how one changed fact affects the analysis;
- synthesize multiple authorities without flattening disagreement;
- communicate a conclusion with calibrated uncertainty;
- recognize professional-responsibility and ethical constraints.

The ABA's outcomes framework emphasizes substantive and procedural law, legal
analysis/reasoning and problem solving, research and communication, ethics, and
professional skills. Use those capabilities to choose coverage rather than
memorizing case names or rule lists by default.

## Rule and element cards

Break a rule into independently gradable elements only when each element must be
retrieved separately. Also include at least one card that reconstructs the rule's
structure or applies it to a minimal fact pattern. State exceptions and burdens
when they change the outcome.

Avoid clozing long rule paragraphs verbatim. The learner should retrieve legal
relationships, not the source's prose. Quote exact statutory or constitutional
language only when exact wording is itself the justified target and lawful to
reproduce.

## Cases and authorities

A case card should earn its review cost through a usable proposition or
reasoning move. Potentially separate:

- legally decisive facts;
- issue and procedural posture;
- holding at the correct level of generality;
- governing rationale or test;
- limiting fact, later treatment, or dissent when instructionally important;
- transfer to a new hypothetical.

Do not use “What happened in Case X?” as an unbounded prompt. Do not present a
headnote, syllabus, or AI summary as the court's holding without checking the
opinion and subsequent authority.

## Hypotheticals and issue spotting

Use short original hypotheticals that vary one legally significant fact at a
time before progressing to mixed issues. Ask for the issue, controlling rule,
decisive fact, argument, counterargument, or likely result—not all of IRAC in one
card.

Then use longer writing or oral practice outside SRS to integrate issues. A
flashcard can maintain the components of legal analysis but cannot replace
research, briefing, counseling, negotiation, or timed exam writing.

## LSAT and admissions-test decks

Keep test-preparation claims separate from substantive law. Verify the current
format, timing, scored sections, and official terminology against LSAC before
each audit.

Cards should teach transferable reasoning moves:

- identify premises, conclusion, assumptions, and argumentative role;
- test conditional and quantified claims without adding unwarranted converses;
- find the gap an answer must strengthen, weaken, justify, or exploit;
- distinguish what is stated, implied, compatible, and unsupported;
- map passage structure, viewpoints, evidence, and author attitude;
- diagnose why a tempting answer is wrong.

Use original micro-arguments and passages. Do not copy or closely paraphrase
copyrighted LSAC questions, answer choices, or explanations. Full timed sections
and official item practice belong in licensed LSAC materials; SRS should
maintain reasoning patterns and error diagnoses.

## Figures and representations

High-value visuals include authority hierarchies, procedural timelines,
argument maps, element/fact matrices, claim-evidence maps, conditional diagrams,
and relationships among opinions. A diagram must preserve direction, burden,
sequence, and uncertainty; visual neatness must not imply a legal conclusion the
authority does not support.

## Accuracy and audit checks

- Verify rule, jurisdiction, effective date, and subsequent treatment.
- Cite primary authority for substantive claims and label secondary summaries.
- Separate descriptive law from normative policy arguments.
- Preserve standards such as “reasonable,” “substantial,” or “material” rather
  than replacing them with false precision.
- Check whether exceptions, burdens, remedies, or procedural posture change the
  answer.
- Identify contested questions and circuit/state splits rather than inventing
  one universal rule.
- Remove outdated test-format and admissions-policy cards promptly.

## Practice outside SRS

Use official question sets, timed sections, case reading, research, briefing,
issue-spotting essays, rule synthesis, citation work, and oral/written argument.
Cards support but do not substitute for these performances.
